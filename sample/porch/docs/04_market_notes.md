# Market Notes

## Competitive Landscape

**Who else is in this space? How are you different?**

Porch sits at the intersection of online grocery, local food marketplaces, and last-mile logistics. The market has demand but no dominant, nationwide product that preserves farmers-market economics while offering modern convenience.

| Competitor | Stage | What They Do | What They Miss |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Good Eggs** | Growth-stage private company | Regional delivery of high-quality local/organic groceries | Retailer model with warehousing and inventory risk; difficult unit economics outside core metros |
| **Instacart / DoorDash / Uber Eats grocery** | Public / late-stage | Fast grocery delivery from retailers | Not farm-direct; limited vendor identity; same distributor-led assortment |
| **CSA subscriptions** | Fragmented legacy channel | Weekly direct subscription from individual farms | No cross-vendor cart, low flexibility, high churn when product mix doesn’t fit household needs |
| **Local farm co-ops / buying clubs** | Regional nonprofit/small private | Aggregate local producers for pickup | Operationally fragmented, poor UX, limited discovery and scheduling flexibility |
| **Facebook/Instagram DM ordering** | Informal channel | Lightweight vendor-to-customer ordering | Not scalable, no routing, no standardized payments, no multi-vendor checkout |
| **Thrive Market / Misfits / Imperfect** | Scale digital grocery | Online grocery with value positioning | Not local-market-native; sourced through distribution layers |

**What are incumbents missing?**

Large grocery delivery incumbents optimize for SKU breadth and speed from existing retail infrastructure, not authenticity of farm-direct supply. Their economics rely on centralized inventory and distributor relationships, which reduces farmer margins and weakens local differentiation.

Most alternatives also fail to solve the core behavioral challenge: customers want flexibility, not rigid subscriptions, and vendors want incremental demand without changing harvest or market-day operations.

**Who are the new entrants?**

- **Vertical local-food startups** in select cities that bundle CSA + delivery.
- **Municipal/regional food-tech pilots** funded by local economic development grants.
- **Restaurant-focused farm marketplaces** expanding into household delivery.

Porch’s advantage is positioning as a **marketplace coordination layer**: no inventory ownership, no warehouse dependency, and no requirement for vendor exclusivity.

## Market Sizing

### TAM — Total Addressable Market

Porch’s TAM is US spending that can reasonably shift to farm-direct, multi-vendor local food purchasing through digital ordering + delivery.

- US farmers market direct-to-consumer sales: ~$12B annually.
- Adjacent spend from local-preferring households currently purchasing from premium grocery: substantial and growing.
- If Porch captures both shifted farmers-market demand and convenience-driven expansion, practical TAM extends beyond existing market-day throughput.

**TAM estimate: ~$18B–$25B (US, medium-term)**

### SAM — Serviceable Addressable Market

Initial SAM focuses on households and vendors in metro areas where Porch can operationally launch with reliable weekly delivery density.

Assumptions:
1. 20 target metros in first multi-year phase.
2. 1.2M–1.8M local-food-interested households across those metros.
3. Realistic annual spend through Porch of $1,200–$2,000 per active household.

**SAM estimate: ~$1.4B–$3.0B GMV opportunity**

### SOM — Serviceable Obtainable Market

Three-year obtainable share based on pilot learnings, vendor onboarding velocity, and city-by-city rollout constraints:

- Year 1: 1 metro at depth + 1 expansion pilot, $2M–$4M GMV.
- Year 2: 4–6 active metros, $12M–$20M GMV.
- Year 3: 10–12 active metros, $40M–$70M GMV.

**SOM (3-year): ~$40M–$70M GMV**, with take-rate monetization converting to platform revenue.

## Pricing Benchmarks

Current market benchmarks:
- Grocery marketplaces often take **15–35%** merchant margin equivalent through commissions, ad spend, and fulfillment fees.
- CSA/payment processors effectively impose hidden operational margin costs through labor and churn.
- Farm-to-retailer wholesale channels can compress farmer realized value significantly versus direct sales.

**Porch pricing philosophy:**
- Preserve vendor economics with an **80/20 split** (vendor/Porch) as a core trust primitive.
- Add transparent customer delivery/service fee calibrated to route economics.
- Avoid hidden penalties and lock-in.

Indicative model:
- **Platform take rate:** 20% of order subtotal.
- **Customer delivery fee:** variable by zone/order window.
- **Optional premium membership:** future layer for free/discounted delivery and loyalty benefits.

## Buyer Behavior

**Customer purchase process:**
1. Discover weekly vendor inventory by neighborhood/market.
2. Build a mixed cart across 3–8 vendors.
3. Choose delivery window or pickup option.
4. Reorder from prior baskets with seasonal substitutions.

**Vendor adoption process:**
1. Onboard catalog + weekly listing workflow.
2. Set quantity commitments before ordering window closes.
3. Fulfill committed inventory on delivery day with no warehouse handoff.
4. Review customer demand and reorder patterns week over week.

**Budget owner:**
- Household grocery budget holder (typically 30–55, dual-income or convenience-sensitive).
- On vendor side, owner/operator of farm or artisan business.

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Customers: quality, reliability, delivery convenience, and transparent provenance.
- Vendors: net revenue retained, operational complexity, predictability of demand.

## Category Trends

1. **Local-first food preferences remain elevated.** Post-pandemic buying behavior showed sustained demand for local sourcing and supply transparency.
2. **Last-mile APIs have improved startup economics.** Third-party courier infrastructure now enables lower capex go-to-market than building a driver fleet.
3. **Small-vendor software adoption increased.** More farms now use Square/Shopify-compatible workflows, reducing onboarding friction.
4. **Consumers expect digital discovery before purchase.** Browsing inventory before leaving home is now baseline behavior in grocery.

## Market Timing Rationale

**Why now and not earlier?**

**Too early in 2018–2020:** Last-mile costs and operational tooling made local-food delivery hard to scale without warehousing.

**Too late beyond 2028 risk:** If national incumbents deeply verticalize “local” assortments with better provenance and vendor economics, differentiation narrows.

**Right now:** Demand is established, infrastructure is available, and supplier pain is acute. Porch can win by owning the multi-vendor local-food convenience layer before the category is consolidated.
