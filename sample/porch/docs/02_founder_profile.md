# Founder Profile

## Background

**Share your journey — what led you here?**

I'm Daniela Reyes, CEO and co-founder of Porch. I grew up in Sacramento, California, where my mother ran a small herb and specialty pepper farm that sold exclusively at the Sacramento Central Farmers Market every Saturday. I spent most of my childhood at that market — waking up at 4:30am, loading the truck, watching my mother negotiate with customers, watching her drive home with boxes of wilted basil she hadn't been able to sell. The waste was constant. The opportunity was also constant: she had regulars who would have ordered every week if they could, but the market was the only channel.

After UC Davis (food science, minor in economics), I spent four years at Instacart — first as an operations analyst on the San Francisco market team, then as a product manager on the retailer integration team. I helped onboard Whole Foods and Safeway onto the platform and spent two years thinking deeply about the gap between what those retailers carried and what consumers actually wanted. The data was unambiguous: demand for local, farm-direct produce was high and growing; supply was locked in a distribution model that couldn't serve it.

My co-founder is Ryan Nakamura, CTO. Ryan was the founding engineer at Farmstead, a Bay Area local grocery delivery startup that reached $8M ARR before being acquired by Good Eggs in 2021. He spent two years post-acquisition at Good Eggs as Head of Platform, where he watched the warehouse model buckle under inventory costs and spoilage. He left convinced that the right architecture for local food delivery was marketplace-not-retailer — exactly what Porch is built on.

**What is your personal connection to the problem?**

I watch my mother's farm every year. She is now doing $180K/year at two markets in Sacramento. She has a waitlist of restaurants that want to buy from her. She turns away CSA inquiries because she doesn't have the administrative bandwidth to run a CSA. Every summer she loses 10–15% of her harvest to spoilage because the Saturday market traffic wasn't there. I built Porch for her. The fact that there are 85,000 farms like hers in the US makes it a business.

**What were you doing before this?**

After Instacart, I joined a seed-stage food-tech startup (Harvest Table, a meal-kit company focused on locally-sourced ingredients) as Head of Supply Chain. We scaled from 3 to 9 cities in 18 months before running into the fundamental problem of local food logistics at scale: you can't warehouse it efficiently. Harvest Table pivoted to a national ingredient model. I didn't follow. That experience clarified exactly what the right model had to look like: no warehousing, no inventory risk, just pure coordination between farms and households.

I left Harvest Table in January 2025, spent two months talking to 80 farmers market vendors and 120 customers across four cities, and incorporated Porch in March 2025. Ryan joined as co-founder in April. We ran our first pilot market — Portland, Oregon — starting in June 2025.

## Why This Problem?

**What makes you uniquely positioned to solve this?**

My unfair advantage is supply-side access. I know how farms work because I grew up on one. I know their economics, their harvest rhythms, their distrust of platforms that extract too much margin, and the specific operational ask that is and isn't feasible on a Thursday night after a harvest day. Most marketplace founders think about the demand side. I think first about vendor onboarding — what makes a farm feel safe committing inventory to a platform they don't control yet. That intuition is not something you can build from a spreadsheet.

Ryan's advantage is that he has already built this infrastructure once. He wrote the order management, driver routing, and vendor dashboard code at Farmstead. At Good Eggs he saw exactly where the warehouse model breaks and why. Porch's technical architecture reflects years of hard-won lessons about what works at the unit-economics level for local food delivery.

Together: I own supply (vendor relationships, onboarding, farmer trust) and Ryan owns the platform. We have complementary networks across the farm community, the Instacart/DoorDash operations community, and the food-tech VC community.

**Why are you the right person for this?**

I have now spent ten cumulative years thinking about why local food distribution is broken and watching every proposed solution either overfit the demand side (CSAs, subscriptions) or collapse under warehouse economics (Good Eggs, FreshDirect local lines). I have a specific, testable thesis about why the marketplace model — where Porch coordinates but never warehouses — is the unit-economic unlock. Ryan has already built a version of this and learned from its failure modes at Good Eggs.

We also have what most food-tech founders don't: farmer trust. In our Portland pilot, we onboarded 22 of the 31 vendors we approached in the first six weeks. The average platform-to-vendor conversion in comparable marketplaces is 30–40%. Ours was 71%. That number is a direct consequence of Daniela's background and the terms we offer — 80% revenue share, no exclusivity, no warehousing requirement, no weekly minimums.

**What is your unfair advantage?**

1. **Supply-side network:** Through my mother's network, my time in Sacramento farm circles, and Ryan's relationships from Farmstead/Good Eggs, we have warm introductions to farm operators in 11 of our target 15 cities. We don't cold-call vendors.
2. **Operational playbook from Farmstead/Good Eggs:** Ryan has already built and stress-tested the logistics stack for same-day local food delivery. We are not writing the vendor intake, routing, or spoilage-tracking systems from scratch — we are refining them.
3. **Portland pilot data:** 6 months of real operational data. 22 vendors, 340 weekly active customers, 87% order retention rate week-over-week. $34K GMV in our peak week. These are not projections — they are receipts.
4. **Differentiated vendor terms:** 80/20 revenue share (vs. industry standard 60/40 or worse). No exclusivity. Vendors can participate in Porch and still run their market table the same day. This is the single biggest unlock for adoption — we are not asking farms to bet on us.
